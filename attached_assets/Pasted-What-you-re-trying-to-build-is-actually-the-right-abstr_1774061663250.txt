What you’re trying to build is actually the right abstraction: not “replace GPT/Claude,” but wrap them with a persistent, personalized decision layer. Training a small model can work—but it’s usually not the first or highest-leverage move.

Let me break this down in a way that aligns with how strong AI systems are actually built today.

⸻

1) Why base LLMs fail for your use case

Models like GPT-5.3, Claude Opus, and Gemini fail here for 3 structural reasons:

1. No persistent identity

They don’t remember your:
	•	tone preferences (direct vs diplomatic)
	•	risk tolerance (ignore vs escalate)
	•	business context (who matters, who doesn’t)

2. Weak decision boundaries

Email tasks are not just generation:
	•	classify (urgent / ignore / delegate)
	•	decide intent
	•	choose action
LLMs are generative, not deterministic decision engines.

3. Prompt ≠ personality

You can try prompting:

“Act like me…”

…but it’s:
	•	brittle
	•	inconsistent across sessions
	•	hard to evolve

⸻

2) The architecture you actually want

Instead of “train a small model,” think:

Layered Personal AI System

                │  Base LLM (GPT etc)│
                └─────────┬──────────┘
                          │
        ┌─────────────────▼─────────────────┐

3) Where a small model actually fits

Yes—you should train a small model, but for classification + routing, not writing.

Good use of a small model:
	•	Email classification (priority, intent, sender importance)
	•	Action prediction (reply / ignore / schedule)
	•	Tone selection (formal / blunt / friendly)

Bad use:
	•	Full email drafting (LLMs still dominate here)

⸻

4) Practical implementation strategy

Step 1 — Build a “Preference Memory”

Store structured signals:

{
  "sender": "boss@company.com",
  "priority": "high",
  "reply_style": "concise + assertive",
  "typical_action": "reply within 2h"
}

Use:
	•	embeddings (e.g. FAISS or Weaviate — you already mentioned this 👍)
	•	plus structured rules

⸻

Step 2 — Train a lightweight classifier

Use something like:
	•	DistilBERT / MiniLM
	•	or even logistic regression + embeddings

Train on:
	•	your past emails
	•	labeled with:
	•	priority
	•	action
	•	tone

Output:

{
  "intent": "request",
  "priority": "low",
  "action": "ignore",
  "tone": "neutral"
}


⸻

Step 3 — Style adapter (this is key)

Instead of “training personality,” do:

Prompt templating + constraints

Example:

You are writing as Fang.

Style rules:
- Be concise (<= 3 sentences)
- No unnecessary politeness
- Prefer direct statements
- Avoid filler phrases

Context:
- Sender importance: HIGH
- Relationship: professional

Draft:

This is far more controllable than fine-tuning.

⸻

Step 4 — Feedback loop (critical)

Every time you:
	•	edit a draft
	•	override a classification

Log:

input → model output → your correction

This becomes your training dataset over time.

⸻

5) If you REALLY want to train a small model for personality

You have 3 options:

Option A — Fine-tune a small open model

Examples:
	•	LLaMA (small variants)
	•	Mistral

Train on:
	•	your sent emails only

Pros:
	•	captures tone well

Cons:
	•	expensive to maintain
	•	still worse than large LLMs at reasoning

⸻

Option B — LoRA adapter (recommended)
	•	lightweight fine-tuning
	•	plug into base model

This gives:
	•	“your tone” layer
	•	without retraining everything

⸻

Option C — Embedding-based style retrieval (underrated)

Instead of training:
	•	retrieve 3 similar past emails you wrote
	•	feed them into prompt

This often beats fine-tuning in practice.

⸻

6) What top builders actually do (important insight)

The best systems don’t rely on one method:

They combine:
	1.	Rules (hard constraints)
	2.	Small ML (classification)
	3.	LLM (generation)
	4.	Memory (long-term personalization)

Not:

“Train one model to do everything”

⸻

“LLMs are not good enough”

You haven’t externalized your decision-making process into a system yet

Once you do:
