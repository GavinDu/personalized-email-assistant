That “post-processing layer” is where your assistant stops being a generic LLM and starts behaving like you. Most people underinvest here.

Let’s break it down clearly.

⸻

1) What “tone enforcement + formatting” actually means

After the LLM (e.g. GPT-5.3 or Claude Opus) generates a draft, you don’t trust it fully.

Instead, you run a deterministic layer:

LLM Draft
   ↓
Tone Enforcement (rules + small model)
   ↓
Formatting Normalization
   ↓
Final Output


⸻

2) Why this layer is critical (non-obvious insight)

LLMs are:
	•	probabilistic
	•	inconsistent across time
	•	sensitive to prompt noise

So even with a perfect prompt, you’ll see:
	•	extra politeness sometimes
	•	too verbose replies occasionally
	•	tone drift depending on context

👉 Post-processing = consistency layer

⸻

3) Tone enforcement: how it actually works

You can implement this in 3 progressively stronger levels

⸻

Level 1 — Rule-based rewriting (start here)

You define hard constraints:

Example rules:

- max 3 sentences
- no phrases like:
  "Hope you're doing well"
  "Just wanted to follow up"
- avoid hedging:
  "maybe", "perhaps", "I think"

Implementation:

def enforce_tone(text):
    text = remove_phrases(text)
    text = limit_sentences(text, max=3)
    text = remove_hedging(text)
    return text


⸻

👉 This alone gives a huge improvement.

⸻

Level 2 — LLM-as-editor (very effective)

Use the LLM again, but only for rewriting, not thinking:

Rewrite the following email to match this style:

- concise
- direct
- no unnecessary politeness
- assertive tone

Keep meaning identical.

Email:
{draft}


⸻

Why this works:
	•	easier task than generation
	•	more controllable
	•	more consistent

⸻

Level 3 — Learned tone model (your “personality model”)

Train a small model that scores tone:

Input:
	•	email text

Output:

{
  "directness": 0.8,
  "politeness": 0.2,
  "verbosity": 0.3
}

Then:
	•	reject / rewrite if outside your thresholds

⸻

4) Formatting layer (often overlooked)

This is fully deterministic and very powerful.

Examples:

Structure normalization
	•	always:
	•	1–3 sentences
	•	no long paragraphs

Signature enforcement

Best,  
Fang

Bullet preference
If email contains multiple points:

Convert to:

- point 1
- point 2


⸻

5) How your tone actually improves over time

This is where your RL loop connects.

⸻

Step 1 — Capture your edits

Every time you change:

LLM draft → your final version

Store both.

⸻

Step 2 — Extract “delta features”

Compute:
	•	length difference
	•	removed phrases
	•	tone shifts

Example:

LLM: "Hope you're doing well, just wanted to check..."
YOU: "Checking on this."

→ learned:
- remove greeting
- compress sentence


⸻

Step 3 — Turn into rules or training data

You’ll see patterns like:
	•	always remove greetings
	•	shorten sentences by ~40%
	•	replace soft language with direct verbs

⸻

Step 4 — Feed back into system
	•	update rule engine
	•	update rewrite prompt
	•	train tone scorer

⸻

6) Advanced pattern (this is very powerful)

“Style retrieval” (better than fine-tuning)

Using ChromaDB:

Before rewriting, retrieve:
	•	3 similar emails you wrote

Then:

Rewrite this email in my style.

Here are examples of my writing:
[examples]

Email:
[draft]


⸻

👉 This makes tone:
	•	adaptive
	•	context-aware
	•	highly personalized

⸻

7) Where most systems fail (important)

They try to:

“get the tone right in one prompt”

Instead of:

generate → enforce → refine

⸻

8) Your ideal pipeline

1. LLM generates draft
2. Retrieve similar past emails (ChromaDB)
3. Rewrite with style conditioning
4. Apply rule-based enforcement
5. Apply formatting normalization
6. (optional) score with tone model
7. output


⸻

9) Key insight (this is the unlock)

Your “personality” is not in:
	•	the base LLM
	•	or even fine-tuning

It emerges from:

constraints + examples + feedback loops
