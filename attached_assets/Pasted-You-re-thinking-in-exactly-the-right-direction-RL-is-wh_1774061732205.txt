You’re thinking in exactly the right direction—RL is what turns your assistant from “smart” into “aligned with you.”
But the key is: don’t jump straight into heavy RL (like PPO). For your email assistant, you want a practical, data-efficient RL loop that works with small models + LLMs.

⸻

1) First principle: what “RL” means in your system

You’re not training a robot—you’re optimizing decisions like:
	•	classify → priority / intent
	•	decide → reply / ignore / schedule
	•	generate → tone / structure

So your RL loop is really:

Learn from your corrections to improve future decisions

⸻

2) The correct architecture (RL applied to your stack)

Incoming Email

  - edit / approve / override

3) Define your RL components (mapped to your use case)

State (S)

Everything the system sees:

{
  "email": "...",
  "sender": "...",
  "thread_context": "...",
  "retrieved_examples": [...]
}


⸻

Action (A)

What your system outputs:

{
  "priority": "high",
  "action": "reply",
  "tone": "direct",
  "draft": "..."
}


⸻

Reward (R) — THIS is the most important

You don’t need fancy reward models. Start simple:

Explicit signals
	•	✅ you send without editing → +1
	•	✏️ you edit → -0.3
	•	❌ you discard → -1

⸻

Implicit signals (very powerful)
	•	response time ↓ → positive
	•	re-edit multiple times → negative
	•	you override classification → negative

⸻

👉 Combine into:

reward = 
  +1.0 * send_without_edit
  -0.3 * edit_distance
  -1.0 * discard
  -0.5 * classification_override


⸻

4) Don’t start with PPO — use this instead

Phase 1 — Supervised learning (most important)

From your logs:

input → model_output → your_final_output

Train:
	•	classifier → on corrected labels
	•	tone selector → on your edits

👉 This alone gets you ~70–80% of the benefit

⸻

Phase 2 — Bandit optimization (THIS is your RL sweet spot)

Instead of full RL, use contextual bandits:

For each email:
	•	try slightly different strategies:
	•	tone A vs tone B
	•	short vs long
	•	observe reward

Update:

P(action | context) ↑ if reward high


⸻

This is:
	•	stable
	•	data-efficient
	•	works great on CPU

⸻

Phase 3 — Preference model (optional, powerful)

Train a small model:

Input:
	•	(draft A, draft B)

Output:
	•	which one you preferred

This is basically RLHF-lite.

⸻

5) Where to apply RL (important separation)

✅ Apply RL to:
	•	classification thresholds
	•	action selection (reply vs ignore)
	•	tone choice
	•	prompt strategy

⸻

❌ Don’t apply RL directly to:
	•	full text generation (too complex)

Let:
	•	GPT-5.3 / Claude Opus handle generation

⸻

7) Simple bandit example (very practical)

pick tone = argmax(expected_reward + exploration)
